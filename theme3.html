Chapter III

Reframe Everything

On perspective shifts, cognitive reappraisals, and new seeing

Of all the tools I encountered in this course, reframing may be the most portable: the one that requires no special setting, no group, no framework. Just the willingness to ask, what if I thought about this differently? From replacing "I have to" with "I get to," to recognizing that risk-takers believe in their own agency rather than luck, to discovering that an unresolved brainstorm can still be a successful one, this theme is about the subtle but compounding power of choosing a different lens. Perspective is a skill. And like any skill, it improves with practice.

"Progress cannot be made if we forget our past, and the same is true of creative work."
On the Sankofa principle, from artist Steve Prince's visit to class
Three Lessons
Lesson 01

"I Have To" Becomes "I Get To"

Guest speaker Gene Roche introduced a reframe so small it almost seemed too simple: instead of "I have to do this assignment," try "I get to do this assignment." The shift is grammatical, but the effect is significant. College is hard. Post-graduation feels daunting. The obligations pile up, especially in a final semester when senioritis competes with everything else. But I am here, at a school I love, taking classes I chose, which is a privilege I let myself forget on a regular basis. The cognitive reappraisal doesn't erase the difficulty. It changes your relationship to it. Assignments become opportunities. Challenges become evidence that you're somewhere worth being.

How I'm Applying This

I've already started doing this, and even the imperfect version works. When I catch myself saying "I have to" about something I actually chose, I rewrite the sentence. It doesn't erase the difficulty. It changes my relationship to it.

Lesson 02

Look Back to Move Forward

Artist Steve Prince introduced us to the Sankofa bird, a figure from Ghanaian tradition depicted with its head turned backward even as it moves forward, carrying an egg in its beak. The image is a reminder that progress does not require abandoning the past. Prince himself embodied this: his artwork drew on his upbringing, his research, his history, weaving it all into something new. This resonated with how I think about creative work more broadly. Our experiences, including the difficult ones, are not weight to leave behind. They are context. They are material. The past is not the opposite of forward motion; it is part of its foundation.

How I'm Applying This

I want to start treating my own history as a creative resource rather than just a résumé. Before I start something new, I'm going to ask what from my past is actually relevant here and hasn't been used yet. Experience isn't baggage. It's material.

Lesson 03

Reappraise What Counts as Success

Two observations from late in the semester reframed how I measure outcomes. The first: people who take risks and see them pay off don't believe things just happen to them. They have a genuine sense of personal agency. I have a habit of attributing others' success to luck or timing, and this observation challenged me directly. Luck is usually the visible surface of invisible effort. Reappraising someone's success as the product of intention and hard work doesn't diminish it. It makes it more useful as motivation.

The second: a team brainstorm that ended without a conclusion wasn't a failure. We had asked each other meaningful questions, surfaced angles none of us had individually, and created real momentum. A conversation, a process, a day can be productive without being conclusive. Sometimes the most honest measure of a session is simply whether we built something together that we couldn't have built alone.

How I'm Applying This

I'm working on stopping myself when I chalk someone's success up to luck and asking what choices led there instead. And in group work, I'm trying a new definition of a productive session: not whether we reached a conclusion, but whether we built something we couldn't have built alone.
