Chapter I

Embrace the Unknown

On ambiguity, adaptability, and letting go of control

Creative problem solving begins where certainty ends. Across this semester, I discovered that my discomfort with ambiguity was both a personal habit and a professional liability, and that the willingness to sit with not-knowing is, in itself, a skill worth developing. Whether I was adapting to a chaotic classroom exercise, confronting my own reaction to a self-imposed stressor, or learning that my deepest values could sometimes hold me back, the lesson was consistent: control is often an illusion, and releasing it creates space for something far more interesting.

"My intrinsic desire to succeed was more powerful than my uncertainty."
On learning that not knowing the destination doesn't mean you aren't moving forward
Three Lessons
Lesson 01

Let Go of the Performance

I walked into a class expecting a lecture and instead played games. By the end, I felt more engaged and more creatively alive than I had in weeks. The day worked precisely because it defied my expectations. I let loose, spoke up, and cared far less about how I was being perceived. It echoed the culture I'd seen in a Pixar documentary: a stark contrast to the polished, hierarchical environment of the law firm where I was working at the time.

The same lesson appeared later when guest speakers navigated an unexpected situation with their daughter in tow. They acknowledged it, pivoted, and kept going with complete authenticity. I realized that adaptability isn't what you do when a plan fails. It is a practiced skill that belongs right alongside preparation. Both moments had the same underlying truth: the pressure to appear polished can crowd out the very qualities that make creative thinking possible.

How I'm Applying This

I work best when I stop performing competence. In high-stakes rooms, I'm practicing naming what's happening out loud when something goes sideways, then moving through it. Being adaptable has more staying power than being polished.

Lesson 02

Ambiguity Is a Resource, Not a Problem to Solve

When my group was tasked with brainstorming ways to cultivate tolerance for not-knowing, my instinct was to immediately reach for the nearest certainty: a textbook, a framework, a familiar model. I went straight for my Decision Theory notes. That response, I realized, was exactly the problem. My solution to ambiguity was to flee it. Sitting with unresolved tension (in a problem, a conversation, or a creative process) isn't comfortable. But if I close that space off too quickly, I also close off the most interesting possibilities. Divergent thinking requires the willingness to not know the answer before you start.

How I'm Applying This

When I feel the impulse to reach for a familiar framework, I'm trying to pause first. Even five minutes of sitting with the question before I default to what I already know has produced ideas I wouldn't have reached otherwise. My most interesting thinking tends to live in the uncomfortable window before I've figured anything out yet.

Lesson 03

The Obstacle Is Often Internal

Two exercises this semester named something I'd never clearly examined before. In the first, we were asked to identify our biggest "bear" of the week. Mine was planning a graduation party. The moment I said it out loud, the absurdity was revelatory: the perceived magnitude of a problem is shaped far more by mindset than by the situation itself. The stress we stack on top of a problem, the catastrophizing, the urgency we assign it, is often its own separate beast.

In the second, we mapped our core values and then examined how they could hinder us. I had always been taught that acting in accordance with my values leads to success. No one had suggested those same values might be limiting me. My drive for balance, it turned out, sometimes prevents me from prioritizing the things that matter most. I spread my attention equally instead of equitably.

How I'm Applying This

I've started asking myself two questions before reacting to a stressor: is this actually urgent, and how much of this weight did I add myself? For my values, I'm working on treating my tasks equitably rather than equally. Balance doesn't mean giving everything the same attention. It means giving each thing the attention it actually deserves.
